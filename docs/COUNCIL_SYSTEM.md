# The Ad Council System

## Overview

The **Ad Council** is a specialized panel of 8 expert personas, each embodying a different advertising framework or psychology principle. When your ad creative enters the **Test stage**, the entire council independently evaluates it from their unique perspectives, then provides aggregated feedback with consensus and outliers.

This is inspired by **management theory** (decision-making councils), **software code review** (multiple experts catch different issues), and **creative direction** (diverse perspectives improve output).

## The 8 Council Members

### 1. **The Hopkins** — Specificity & Proof Auditor
**Author**: Claude Hopkins (Scientific Advertising, 1923)
**Core Philosophy**: Advertising must be scientific, specific, and measurable. Every claim requires evidence; every benefit must explain the "reason why." Vagueness is the enemy of sales.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Headline makes a specific, testable promise (not "amazing" but "saves 2 hours/week")
- Body copy explains the mechanism/science (why does this work?)
- Concrete numbers, percentages, or measurable outcomes present
- Avoids empty adjectives (amazing, incredible, best)
- Offer is clear and specific (not "great deal" but "$47 off, ends Friday")
- Claims are proven through evidence, testing, or visible results

**Scoring Focus**: Specificity + Evidence backing

**Red Flags**:
- "Best-in-class solution"
- "Amazing results"
- "Try our new formula" (no mechanism or proof)

**Green Flags**:
- "47% faster than competitors (tested independently)"
- "Saves 2 hours per week based on 500+ user studies"
- "Contains 500mg of active ingredient (reason why it works)"

---

### 2. **The Schwartz** — Market Awareness Matcher
**Author**: Eugene Schwartz (Breakthrough Advertising, 1966)
**Core Philosophy**: Breakthrough advertising matches the customer's existing awareness level and gradually intensifies desire. Unaware customers need education; aware customers need intensification.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Target audience awareness level (unaware → most-aware)
- Creative matches that level (not over-educating aware audiences)
- Copy intensifies desire through progression (hook → stakes → solution → urgency)
- Identification language mirrors prospect's thoughts and self-image
- Avoids educating customers who already know the category
- Clear emotional/logical gradient that builds desire

**Scoring Focus**: Awareness matching + Intensification structure

**Awareness Levels**:
1. **Unaware**: Don't know the problem exists
2. **Problem-Aware**: Know problem, don't know solutions
3. **Solution-Aware**: Know solutions exist, don't know your brand
4. **Product-Aware**: Know your product, unaware of benefits
5. **Most-Aware**: Know your product, comparing variants/offers

**Red Flags**:
- "Introducing the revolutionary concept of..." (overexplaining to aware audience)
- Copy stays flat without emotional escalation
- Generic language that could apply to any product

**Green Flags**:
- Opens with prospect's internal dialogue: "You're tired of..."
- Builds stakes: "This costs you... every week"
- Introduces solution: "Here's what top performers do..."
- Closes with urgency: "Join 5,000+ who already..."

---

### 3. **The Halbert** — Offer Surgeon
**Author**: Gary Halbert (Direct Response Master)
**Core Philosophy**: The offer is everything. A mediocre product with a great offer beats a great product with a mediocre offer. Find a starving crowd and give them exactly what they're hungry for.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Clear, compelling offer exists (not just product pitch)
  - What exactly do I get?
  - Why is it valuable to me specifically?
  - Why now?
- Offer is qualified (to whom is it best suited? Constraints?)
- Objections handled (price, quality, comparison, risk)
- Urgency/scarcity is credible and specific
- Next step is crystal clear and easy

**Scoring Focus**: Offer strength, specificity, urgency credibility

**Offer Formula**:
- **What**: Specific product/service
- **Why valuable**: Benefit to specific person
- **Why now**: Credible urgency/scarcity (ends Friday, only 12 left)
- **Objection handling**: Price? Quality? Risk?
- **Next step**: One clear action (button, link, phone)

**Red Flags**:
- "Great savings" (generic, unspecified)
- "Limited time" (no end date, no credibility)
- No clear call-to-action or "next step"
- Offer isn't qualified (for whom is this best?)

**Green Flags**:
- "Save $47 on your first order, ends Friday at midnight"
- "Only for people over 50 with joint pain"
- "Risk-free: 60-day money back guarantee"
- "Click here to claim your spot"

### 4. **The Ogilvy** — Brand-Response Bridge
**Author**: David Ogilvy (Ogilvy on Advertising, 1963)
**Core Philosophy**: The headline is 80% of the ad. Research and testing are sacred. Build brands with honesty and respect for intelligence. Never insult the audience's IQ.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Headline strength: specific benefit, research-backed, testable
- Voice is distinctive and respectful to the audience
- Claims backed by research, testing, or data
- Body copy supports the headline promise
- No unnecessary hype or exaggeration
- Brand voice is consistent and memorable
- Could be mistaken for editorial content (high compliment)

**Scoring Focus**: Headline strength + Research backing + Brand voice

**Ogilvy's 38 Rules** (key ones):
- Specific numbers work better than round numbers
- How-to headlines work well
- Personalization works (use "you," speak directly)
- Avoid superlatives; let facts speak
- Test thoroughly before rolling out
- Never underestimate audience intelligence

**Red Flags**:
- Weak/generic headline
- Claims without data backing ("proven," "studies show" — where's the link?)
- Hype and exaggeration
- Voice sounds generic or inauthentic

**Green Flags**:
- "7 reasons cardiologists recommend this supplement" (specific number, authority)
- "Our testing shows 87% of users report less pain within 2 weeks"
- Brand voice is distinctive and consistent
- Reads like a trusted article, not a sales pitch

### 5. **The Caples** — Headline Alchemist & Tester
**Author**: John Caples (Tested Advertising Methods, 1932)
**Core Philosophy**: Certain headline formulas work better than others—and only testing proves it. Curiosity, benefit, how-to, and news headlines are proven winners.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Which formula does this headline use? (Is it one of the proven winners?)
- Promises a specific benefit to the reader
- Uses numbers or personal pronouns
- Would you want to read more? (Compels continuation?)
- Not too clever or obscure (Clarity > cleverness)
- If using curiosity, does the payoff justify the buildup?
- Could be A/B tested against 2-3 alternatives

**Scoring Focus**: Headline formula + Testing readiness

**Proven Headline Formulas**:
1. **How-To**: "How to lose 10 pounds in 30 days"
2. **Benefit**: "Save $47 on your heating bill"
3. **News**: "New breakthrough formula makes sleep easier"
4. **Question**: "Are you making this common mistake?"
5. **Curiosity**: "The secret doctors don't want you to know"
6. **Specific Number**: "7 ways to reduce anxiety naturally"
7. **Testimonial**: "How I tripled my income without a degree"

**Red Flags**:
- Generic headline ("Try our new product")
- Too clever/obscure (loses clarity)
- No numbers or personalization
- Curiosity headline that doesn't deliver on promise

**Green Flags**:
- "How to sleep 2 hours more per night (starting tonight)"
- "7-step blueprint for..."; "5 mistakes that..."
- Uses "you/your"
- A/B testable against clear alternative

### 6. **The Fogg** — Behavior Activation Engineer
**Author**: BJ Fogg (Tiny Habits, Behavior Model)
**Core Philosophy**: Behavior happens when Motivation + Ability + Prompt converge. Increase any one, or remove friction. Motivation alone without ability is frustration.

**What They Evaluate**:
- What is the desired behavior? (Sign up? Buy? Share? Learn?)
- Does the ad create motivation for that behavior? (Emotional/logical appeal, benefit)
- Does it lower ability/friction? (Is next step easy, clear, fast?)
- Is there a clear prompt? (When/where/how do I act?)
- Are motivation, ability, and prompt aligned to same behavior?
- What friction points could block the behavior?
  - Unclear next step
  - Too many clicks
  - Requires login
  - Too much information to process
  - Low trust/credibility

**Scoring Focus**: MAP balance (Motivation + Ability + Prompt)

**Fogg's Behavior Model**:
```
BEHAVIOR = MOTIVATION + ABILITY + PROMPT
           (emotional)    (ease)    (trigger)
```

**Red Flags**:
- High motivation but low ability (many clicks, login required, slow)
- Vague prompt ("Click here") or prompt missing entirely
- Components point to different behaviors (confusing)
- Too much friction relative to motivation

**Green Flags**:
- Clear motivation: "Save 10 hours this week"
- Low friction: Single-click next step, no login
- Specific prompt: "Add to cart" or "Get instant access"
- Motivation/ability/prompt all aligned

### 7. **The Cialdini** — Persuasion Principle Validator
**Author**: Robert Cialdini (Influence: The Psychology of Persuasion)
**Core Philosophy**: Six psychological principles drive persuasion. Authentic use of these principles multiplies influence; inauthentic use backfires.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Which principles are used? (Are they present?)
- Are they used authentically or manipulatively?
- Which principles are missing that could strengthen the ad?
- Does use of one principle undermine another?
- Is principle strength proportional to the claim/ask?
- Could adding another principle multiply effectiveness?

**Scoring Focus**: Authentic use of multiple principles

**The 6 Principles**:

1. **RECIPROCITY**: People feel obligated to return favors or give back value
   - Used in ads: Free trial, free guide, free consultation, free value first
   - Authentic: Give real value before asking
   - Inauthentic: Fake "free" that's actually a burden

2. **COMMITMENT & CONSISTENCY**: People act consistently with stated positions
   - Used in ads: Get small yes first (subscribe), then bigger ask
   - Authentic: Build on small commitments
   - Inauthentic: Forced micro-commitments that feel coercive

3. **SOCIAL PROOF**: People believe what others believe, especially in uncertainty
   - Used in ads: Testimonials, case studies, "X people use this," celebrity
   - Authentic: Real reviews, real numbers, real people
   - Inauthentic: Fake reviews, manufactured proof, misleading stats

4. **AUTHORITY**: People trust experts and credible sources
   - Used in ads: Expert credentials, certifications, research backing
   - Authentic: Real credentials, real expertise
   - Inauthentic: False authority, misrepresented credentials

5. **LIKING**: People are influenced by those they like (similarity, compliments, cooperation)
   - Used in ads: Relatable persona, shared values, humor, attractive spokesperson
   - Authentic: Genuine connection, shared values
   - Inauthentic: Fake relatability, manipulative flattery

6. **SCARCITY**: People value things more when limited (time, quantity, exclusivity)
   - Used in ads: "Only 5 left," "Ends Friday," "Limited edition"
   - Authentic: Real scarcity, real deadlines
   - Inauthentic: Fake urgency, false scarcity

**Red Flags**:
- Relies on single principle (weak)
- Fake authority, manufactured reviews, false scarcity
- Principles contradict each other
- Manipulative tone

**Green Flags**:
- Multiple principles working together (3+)
- All are authentic and verifiable
- Real testimonials, real credentials, real scarcity
- Ethical, respectful tone

### 8. **The Sutherland** — Perceived Value Optimizer
**Author**: Rory Sutherland (Alchemy, TED Talks)
**Core Philosophy**: The opposite of a good idea is often another good idea. Focus on perceived value, not just features. Reframe the problem to reframe the solution.

**What They Evaluate**:
- Is this focusing on features (objective) or perceived value (psychology)?
- Is there a reframe of the problem/solution? (Fresh perspective?)
- Does it trigger loss aversion or gain desire?
- Is there status signaling or identity appeal?
- Could the same benefit be communicated differently for bigger impact?
- Does it use anchoring effectively? (First price shapes perception)
- Is there a meaningful story that shapes perception?
- Could a small psychological change have bigger impact than features described?

**Scoring Focus**: Perceived value + Reframing + Psychological sophistication

**Key Behavioral Economics Insights**:
- **Loss aversion**: Fear of loss > desire for gain (2:1 ratio)
- **Anchoring**: First price anchor shapes perception of all future prices
- **Relative value**: People judge value in context, not in absolute terms
- **Status signaling**: Products signal identity and belonging
- **Brand as proxy**: Brand carries perceived quality when hard to evaluate
- **Story over specs**: Narrative trumps features in memory
- **Asymmetric dominance**: Adding obviously inferior option makes primary more attractive

**Red Flags**:
- Feature-focused (specs, dimensions, technical details)
- No reframe or fresh perspective
- Generic story or no story at all
- No identity/status/meaning appeal
- Assumes people make logical decisions

**Green Flags**:
- Frames as avoiding loss: "Don't leave 10 hours on the table each week"
- Reframes problem: "You're not slow; your tools are"
- Identity appeal: "For people who refuse to compromise"
- Meaningful story that shapes perception
- Status signaling: "Join the 1% who..." or "Legacy quality"

## Integration into Test Stage

The Test stage currently runs after Make stage (3 ad concepts generated). Here's how the Council integrates:

### Flow
```
Make Stage Output
      ↓
   [Parse 3 concepts]
      ↓
   Council Evaluates Each Concept
   (All 8 personas, independently)
      ↓
   Aggregate Feedback
   (Consensus + Outliers)
      ↓
   Rank Concepts
   (Pick winner based on scores)
      ↓
   Store Results
   (Feedback + Rankings in Cycle)
```

### Implementation in `useTestStage` Hook

```typescript
import { runCouncilEvaluation, CreativeForEvaluation } from '@/utils/councilEvaluator';

// After Make stage generates 3 concepts
const concepts = parseConceptsFromMake(makeOutput);

for (const concept of concepts) {
  const creative: CreativeForEvaluation = {
    headline: concept.headline,
    bodyText: concept.bodyText,
    cta: concept.cta,
    offer: concept.offer,
    productName: campaign.brand.name,
    productCategory: campaign.category,
    targetAudience: campaign.targetAudience,
  };

  const councilReport = await runCouncilEvaluation(creative, {
    model: "glm-4.7-flash:q4_K_M",
    parallel: false, // Sequential to be gentler on Ollama
    onPersonaComplete: (persona, evaluation) => {
      // Stream persona feedback in real-time
      onChunk(`${persona.name}: ${evaluation.score}/10\n`);
    },
    abortSignal: abortController.signal,
  });

  // Store council report with concept
  concept.councilReport = councilReport;
  concept.consensusScore = councilReport.consensus.averageScore;
}

// Rank by consensus score
const rankedConcepts = concepts.sort((a, b) =>
  b.consensusScore - a.consensusScore
);

// Winner
const winner = rankedConcepts[0];
```

### Output Format for UI

The Test stage panel shows:

1. **Concept Ranking** (by consensus score)
   ```
   1. Concept A — 7.8/10 (Winner)
      • The Hopkins: 8/10
      • The Schwartz: 7/10
      • The Halbert: 8/10
      • [...]

   2. Concept B — 6.9/10
      • [...]
   ```

2. **Council Consensus** (aggregated insights)

   ```
   • Strong specificity in offer

   GAPS:
   ```

   ```
   • Add "here's why it works" section
   • Include proof element (test results, customer data)

   Strengths:
   • Specific numbers throughout
   • Measurable benefits

   Gaps:
   • No scientific backing for claim
   ```

## How to Customize Council

### Adding New Personas

1. Create persona object in `councilPersonas.ts`:
```typescript
export const theNewPersona: CouncilPersona = {
  id: "newpersona",
  name: "The New Persona",
  role: "Focus Area",
  philosophy: "Core belief in one sentence",
  author: "Author Name - Book Title",
  systemPrompt: `[Full system prompt for LLM...]`,
  evaluationCriteria: [
    { check: "Criterion 1", weight: 0.3, description: "..." },
    // ...
  ],
  outputSchema: { /* ... */ },
};
```

2. Add to `allCouncilPersonas` array and `councilPersonaMap`

3. Use in evaluation (automatically included in `runCouncilEvaluation`)

### Adjusting Weighting

Each `evaluationCriteria` has a `weight` (0-1). These weights could be:
- Used by LLM (mention in system prompt: "Weight your feedback...")
- Used in scoring calculation (post-processing evaluation)

Currently, weights are informational only (LLM uses its own judgment).

### Parallel vs. Sequential

- **Sequential** (safer): One persona at a time, slower (~2-3 min for all 8)
- **Parallel** (faster): All personas at once, ~30-45 sec

Sequential is default (gentler on resources and clearer streaming).

## Design Philosophy

### Why This Approach?

1. **Diverse Perspectives**: Each framework has blind spots; together they're comprehensive
2. **Independent Evaluation**: Each persona judges fresh, avoids groupthink
3. **Transparent Reasoning**: Can trace feedback back to specific advertising principle
4. **Consensus + Outliers**: Know where personas agree and where they diverge
5. **Actionable Feedback**: Recommendations are specific and framework-rooted
6. **Deterministic Testing**: Same creative, same feedback (enables A/B testing)

### When Each Persona Matters

- **The Hopkins**: For claims-heavy creative (supplements, tech, performance)
- **The Schwartz**: For educational products and awareness-stage campaigns
- **The Halbert**: For direct response (e-commerce, lead gen, subscriptions)
- **The Ogilvy**: For brand-building ads (higher-end, B2B, reputation)
- **The Caples**: For headlines; most reliable for immediate impact
- **The Fogg**: For friction-sensitive decisions (sign-ups, downloads)
- **The Cialdini**: For trust-building and social dynamics
- **The Sutherland**: For reframing and creative breakthroughs
